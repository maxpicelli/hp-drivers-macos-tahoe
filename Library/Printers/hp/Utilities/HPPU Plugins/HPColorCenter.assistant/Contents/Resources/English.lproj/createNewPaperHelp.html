Step 1 - Define Paper Preset
The paper preset name will show up in the printer driver and the front panel.

Paper type selection is fundamental for good results. The available types are:

  • Bond and Coated Paper: Fiber-based, thin papers with a matte surface. These papers use matte black ink, no gloss enhancer, relatively low ink limits, and high carriage position.
  • Photo Paper: Photo-base, relatively thick papers with variety of finishing from matte to satin to glossy. These papers use photo black ink and gloss enhancer with the exception of Photo Matte Papers, relatively high ink limits, and low carriage position, with the exception of Baryte and Photo Matte Papers. Generally they will print with the starwheels up.
  • Proofing Paper: A variety of papers from matte to satin and glossy used to simulate commercial printing with high color accuracy requirements.
  • Fine Art Material: Canvas, matte and paper base photo materials used for high quality fine art printing. These papers use matte black ink and no gloss enhancer with the exception of Canvas Satin and Pearl papers, which uses photo black and gloss enhancer. They all use high carriage position with high ink limits.
  • Film: Synthetic film base materials that generally use photo black and no gloss enhancer, with relatively low ink limits and high carriage position.
  • Technical Paper: Synthetic translucent materials that use photo black and no gloss enhancer with high ink limits, high carriage position and starwheels up.

HP papers in the list use fine tuned ink limits while the others use generic ink limits. Ink limits for both types can be adjusted using the "Change Printing Properties" button.

Some paper types are available with different ink limits (Less/Normal/More Ink). Ink limits are generally proportional to color gamut volume, but higher ink limit can cause printing artifacts (bleed, coalescence, etc) in some cases.

The "Change Printing Properties" button can be used to modify printing properties after selecting the paper type:

  • Paper Thickness
  • Dry Time
  • Starwheels Up/Down
  • Cutter Enable/Disable
  • Global Ink Volume
  • Gloss Enhancer Amount

Choosing the correct printing properties:

  • Paper Thickness: refers to carriage height or platen gap. You should choose high if your paper weights over 250gr or if it does not have plastic coating.
  • Dry time: the recommendation is using optional by default and moving to extended if needed.
  • Starwheels: print with the starwheels up or down.
  • Cutter: Disable or enable cutter.
  • Global Ink Volume/Gloss Enhancer Amount: initially it can be left as it is and modify it later on, based on printing results. (see point 3)

When an option is grayed-out it is not available for the selected paper type.

In order to optimize printing parameters, the following steps are recommended:

  1. Choose paper type and advanced settings
  2. Perform a calibration
  3. Print an image of your choice, where you can check for color gamut, coalescence, mottle, bronzing and gloss uniformity.
  4. If the results are not satisfactory, repeat the process, paying special attention to:
    • For better color gamut increase ink volume OR select a "more ink" variant of the same paper type if available.
    • To reduce coalescence decrease ink volume and/or gloss enhancer amount OR select a "less ink" variant of the same paper if available.
    • To reduce bronzing or improve gloss uniformity adjust gloss enhancer amount.

Step 2 - Calibrate
Printer calibration is needed to produce repeatable color for your printer, ink and paper combination. It works by linearizing your printer and determining the maximum achievable density for each of the inks. The process consists of printing and measuring patches of different densities of each ink individually.
