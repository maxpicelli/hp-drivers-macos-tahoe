Step 1 - Define Paper Preset
The paper preset name will show up in the printer driver and the front panel.

Paper type selection is fundamental for good results. The available types are:

  • Bond and Coated Paper: For economical, high-quality printing with sharp text and lines. Specially engineered for high-production environments and short-term signage.
  • Photo Paper: Photographic Papers include a variety of substrates for a broad range of applications from high-end fine art photography-where quality is of utmost importance-to photo enlargements, displays, and large-format retail graphics that need to be handled right off the printer.
  • Proofing Paper: Use Proofing Papers to ensure color accuracy for professional-looking comps prior to large-run printing.
  • Fine Art Material: Art papers and canvas materials are designed for art reproduction professionals and offer a wide range of color, texture, thickness, sheen, and durability.
  • Film: Films are optimized to provide sharp lines on plots for archiving, future multiple reproductions, and overlays. Graphic polyester films provide the ideal solution by producing sharp, vivid image quality.
  • Backlit: Backlit Materials are specially developed products for illuminated backlit displays in indoor and outdoor locations.
  • Technical Paper: Designed for architecture (AEC), mechanical drawings (MCAD), and Geographic Information Systems (GIS). Technical Papers produce sharp black text, fine lines, and medium-density area fills.
  • Banner & Sign Material: Plastic (generally pvc, polyester or polypropylene) sign materials for a variety of applications from indoor and outdoor banners and pop-up displays to decorating indoor and outdoor signs.
  • Self-Adhesive Material: Self-Adhesive Materials offer a wide variety of products including coated paper, vinyl, polypropylene, and polyester films, as well as laminates for post-processing.

HP papers in the list use fine-tuned ink limits while the others use generic ink limits. Ink limits for both types can be adjusted using the "Change Printing Properties" button.

Some paper types are available with different ink limits (Less/Normal/More Ink). Ink limits are generally proportional to color gamut volume, but a higher ink limit can cause printing artifacts (bleed, coalescence, etc.) in some cases.

The "Change Printing Properties" button can be used to modify printing properties after selecting the paper type:

  • Paper Thickness
  • Dry Time
  • Starwheels Up/Down
  • Cutter Enable/Disable
  • Global Ink Volume
  • Gloss Enhancer Amount

Choosing the correct printing properties:

  • Paper Thickness: This is relevant to carriage height or platen gap. You should choose high if your paper weighs over 250 g/m² or if it does not have plastic coating.
  • Dry Time: The recommendation is to use optional by default and move to extended if needed.
  • Starwheels: Print with the starwheels up or down.
  • Cutter: Disable or enable the cutter.
  • Global Ink Volume/Gloss Enhancer Amount: Initially it can be left as it is and modified later on, based on printing results (see point 3).

When an option is grayed out it is not available for the selected paper type.

In order to optimize printing parameters, the following steps are recommended:

  1. Choose paper type and advanced settings
  2. Perform a calibration
  3. Print an image of your choice, so that you can check for color gamut, coalescence, mottle, bronzing and gloss uniformity.
  4. If the results are not satisfactory, repeat the process, bearing in mind:
    • For better color gamut, increase the ink volume or select a "more ink" variant of the same paper type if available.
    • To reduce coalescence, decrease the ink volume and/or the amount of gloss enhancer or select a "less ink" variant of the same paper if available.
    • To reduce bronzing or improve gloss uniformity, adjust the amount of gloss enhancer.

Step 2 - Choose ICC Profile Name

The ICC profile name will show up in applications such as Adobe Photoshop. The paper preset name will show up in the printer driver and the front panel. For the profile name, you are recommended to use a combination of the printer model and the paper preset name. For example: "HP Z3200 Fine Art Pearl".

For papers that support gloss enhancer, you must also decide whether to enable or disable it.

Step 3 - Calibrate
Printer calibration is needed to produce repeatable color for your printer, ink and paper combination. It works by linearizing your printer and determining the maximum achievable density for each of the inks. The process consists of printing and measuring patches of different densities of each ink individually.

Step 4 - Profile
An ICC color profile is needed to produce accurate color for your printer, ink and paper combination. It is used to map application colors to printer colors. The process consists of printing and measuring patches of combinations of all inks.

Step 5 - Install Profile
The installation process ensures that the ICC profile will be available to applications such as Adobe Photoshop.
